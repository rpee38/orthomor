Manulex_Morpho v.2. - General description

Manulex_morpho provides information on the frequency and consistency of G-Ph and Ph-G correspondences for a subset of words from the Manulex database (approximatively 10,000 words). The latter indexes all the words included in a selection of Grade 1 to 5 school textbooks published and available in France (Lété, Sprenger-Charolles, & Colé, 2004). The Manulex_morpho database indexes 20% of the lexical entries (i.e: the denominator reflects each word in the corpus, counted once) in the textbooks, which represents more than 90% of the textual entries (i.e:, the denominator reflects all the words in the corpus, including their reoccurrence in the textbooks).

Contrary to other databases published (Manulex_infra; Peereman, Lété, & Sprenger-Charolles, 2007), Manulex_morphoaccounts for the morphological structure of written words, which represents its most valuable contribution to the field. Four categories of morphological markers were used to index graphemes and phonemes in the database, mostly located in at the ends of words: 1. Nominal inflections marking gender and number; 2. Verbal inflections; 3. Morpho-graphemes such as 'ent' in adverbs (rarement, vivement, vraiment); 4 Final consonants in a word, which are silent but can be pronounced when inflected (for example 'd' in 'grand-grande-grandeur').

For each word, the Manulex_morpho database provide the G-Ph and Ph-G frequency and consistency depending on their position in words: initial (first grapheme/phoneme), final (last grapheme/phoneme), and intermediate (graphemes/phonemes in the middle of words). It also provides two types of mean consistency and frequency, regardless of position in a word: one lexical and the other textual. The lexical frequency (i.e. Count by-type) reflects the number of words in the database that include a G-Ph and Ph-G correspondences, whereby each word is only counted once. Text frequency (i.e. Count by-token) reflects the number of words in the texts that include a correspondence of interest, whereby a word is strictly counted as often as it appears in the corpus. For example: In the database, there are 13 words that include the G-Ph correspondence 'ch' - /k/, therefore its lexical frequency is 13. The total sum of occurrences of those 13 words in the corpus of text is 136, and this is therefore the textual frequency of G-Ph and Ph-G correspondences. Note that a large number of alternative pronunciations to the grapheme 'ch' exist in French: 'ch' occurs in an additional 565 words in the database, with a cumulative frequency of 18,289. The consistency of 'ch' - /k/ is therefore fairly low in French. In the database, consistency is expressed as a percentage, obtained from the ratio of the correspondence’s own frequency over the grapheme’s overall frequency. For 'ch' - /k/ lexical consistency is 2.25% (13/[565 + 13]) x 100 = 2.25%) and textual consistency – derived via the same calculation only using textual frequency data – is 0.74% (136/[18 289 + 136]) x 100 = 0.74%).
